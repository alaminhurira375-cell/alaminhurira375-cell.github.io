Research

Food Policy, Nutrition Assistance, and Applied Microeconomics

My research examines how public policies and economic conditions shape food security, diet quality, public health, and household well-being. My dissertation focuses on nutrition assistance programs in the United States, including WIC, school meal policy, and food hardship measurement.

Job Market Paper

The Effects of WIC on Household Food Security and the Quality of Food Acquisitions

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 1 / Job Market Paper.

Abstract

We examine the effect of participation in the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) on household food security and the quality of food acquisitions using data from USDA's FoodAPS for 2012–2013. We employ propensity score matching to address biases arising from self-selection into WIC. The Healthy Eating Index is used to measure dietary quality and captures the nutritional adequacy of household food purchases relative to dietary guidance. This study provides new evidence on program effectiveness through the lens of nutrition security, broadly defined as having both adequate food quantity and food quality. The findings offer policy-relevant evidence on how WIC affects food insecurity and dietary outcomes among low-income households.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “The Effects of WIC on Household Food Security and the Quality of Food Acquisitions.” Working paper, University of Georgia.

Dissertation Research

How Would Changes to Community Eligibility Provision Thresholds Affect Child Diet Quality?

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 2 / AAEA 2026 accepted conference paper.

Abstract

This study simulates how Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) eligibility thresholds affect child diet quality, with a focus on schools near the eligibility threshold where participation is most vulnerable to policy changes. We harmonize school-level administrative data on CEP eligibility and participation for academic years 2014–2015 through 2023–2024, validate participation trends against FRAC fact sheets, and link CEP information to school demographic characteristics from the National Center for Education Statistics. To assess dietary implications, we use NHANES dietary intake data from 2013–2023 and estimate individual fixed-effects models linking the share of calories from school food to Healthy Eating Index scores. We then simulate how changes in CEP thresholds may affect school meal access and child diet quality. The project provides policy-relevant evidence for debates over universal free school meals and CEP eligibility rules.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “How Would Changes to Community Eligibility Provision Thresholds Affect Child Diet Quality?” Working paper, University of Georgia.

Measuring the Distance to Food Sufficiency: A Continuous Approach to Food Hardship

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 3.

Abstract

This study examines food hardship using a continuous measure based on self-reported food expenditure shortfalls from the Current Population Survey Food Security Supplement. Conventional food insecurity indicators rely on discrete classifications that mask variation in the severity of deprivation within categories. Following recent work using a resource-gap approach, we construct a normalized needs index by comparing households’ usual food expenditures with their reported shortfalls. Using CPS Food Security Supplement data from 2002 to 2023, we examine temporal trends and compare this continuous measure with standard food insecurity indicators. The analysis highlights differences between binary and continuous measures, particularly during periods of economic change, and reveals substantial heterogeneity in hardship within food-insecure populations.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “Measuring the Distance to Food Sufficiency: A Continuous Approach to Food Hardship.” Working paper, University of Georgia.

Does the Informal Economy Undermine Political Stability? Empirical Evidence from Global Perspectives

Accepted, Economics of Governance. In production.

Abstract

Political stability is a cornerstone of economic development, yet the role of the informal economy in shaping it remains underexplored. This study examines how the informal and formal economies influence political stability across 124 countries from 1996 to 2020, with attention to income-group heterogeneity. Using the method of moments quantile regression alongside advanced econometric techniques, the study finds that the informal economy systematically undermines political stability while the formal economy enhances it, with effects that vary across the political stability distribution and income groups. The findings contribute a distributional perspective to the literature on economic structure and governance.

“Does the Informal Economy Undermine Political Stability? Empirical Evidence from Global Perspectives.” Economics of Governance, accepted and in production.

Peer-Reviewed Publications

The Role of Fintech and the Shadow Economy in Advancing Sustainable Development Across G7 Countries

Sustainable Development.

Abstract

Achieving sustainable development requires balancing economic growth with environmental conservation, but the relationship between informal economic activities and emerging financial technologies in boosting green growth is not well understood. This study examines the impact of the shadow economy and fintech on green growth in G7 countries from 1996 to 2020. Using method of moments quantile regression, with FMOLS, DOLS, and CCR robustness checks, the study finds that the shadow economy negatively affects fintech and green growth, while fintech contributes positively to green growth. The findings suggest that reducing the shadow economy and promoting fintech ecosystems are complementary strategies for advancing sustainable development.

DOI
“The Role of Fintech and the Shadow Economy in Advancing Sustainable Development Across G7 Countries.” Sustainable Development. https://doi.org/10.1002/sd.71287

Relationship between Body Mass Index, Eating Disorders, Internet Addiction, and Social Media Addictions among Adolescents in Bangladesh

Public Health Challenges.

Abstract

This study assesses the relationship between body mass index, eating disorders, internet addiction, and social media addiction among adolescents in Bangladesh. Using a cross-sectional online survey of adolescents aged 13–19, the study analyzes BMI, eating disorder risk, internet addiction, and social media addiction using descriptive statistics, chi-square tests, logistic regression, and correlation analysis. The findings show meaningful associations among internet addiction, social media addiction, eating disorders, and BMI, highlighting the importance of physical activity, reading habits, and parental awareness in adolescent health.

DOI
“Relationship between Body Mass Index, Eating Disorders, Internet Addiction, and Social Media Addictions among Adolescents in Bangladesh.” Public Health Challenges. https://doi.org/10.1002/puh2.70142

Protected Vegetable Crop Production for Long-term Sustainable Food Security

Journal of Scientific Research and Reports, 30(5), 660–669.

Abstract

This article discusses protected vegetable crop production as a strategy for long-term sustainable food security. Protected farming systems, including greenhouse production, can reduce biotic and abiotic stressors and improve productivity in land-constrained settings. The article highlights how protected agriculture can support vegetable production under conditions of rapid urbanization, declining biodiversity, and rising food demand.

Perilous Resurgence of Dengue Fever in Bangladesh: Gender-Based Perspectives on Risk Perception and Adaptation Strategies

Universal Journal of Public Health, 11(5), 751–760.

Abstract

This paper examines gender differences in dengue fever risk perception and adaptation strategies in Bangladesh. Using cross-sectional survey data from urban areas of the Barishal division, the study evaluates whether men and women perceive dengue-related risks differently and whether they respond differently through preventive behavior. The findings show that overall risk perception is low, but women perceive dengue risks more seriously and are more proactive in taking precautionary measures. The study highlights the importance of gender-sensitive public health policy.

DOI
Md. Tanvir Ahmed and Md. Al Amin. 2023. “Perilous Resurgence of Dengue Fever in Bangladesh: Gender-Based Perspectives on Risk Perception and Adaptation Strategies.” Universal Journal of Public Health, 11(5), 751–760.

Beachgoers' Knowledge, Perceptions, and Willingness to Pay for Sustainable Waste Management in Kuakata Sea Beach, Bangladesh

Nature Environment & Pollution Technology, 23(2).

Abstract

This study examines tourists’ knowledge, perceptions, and willingness to pay for sustainable waste management at Kuakata Sea Beach, Bangladesh. Using survey data from 400 respondents and logistic and ordinary least squares regressions, the study finds broad recognition of the need for waste management improvements, with willingness to pay varying by gender and income. The findings provide evidence for designing policies that support sustainable beach waste management.

DOI
Md Al Amin and Md Tanvir Ahmed. 2024. “Beachgoers' Knowledge, Perceptions, and Willingness to Pay for Sustainable Waste Management in Kuakata Sea Beach, Bangladesh.” Nature Environment & Pollution Technology, 23(2).
