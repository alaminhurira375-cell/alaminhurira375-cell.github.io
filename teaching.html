Teaching Philosophy

I view economics teaching as training students to ask clear questions, reason carefully, evaluate evidence, and communicate conclusions with intellectual honesty. In applied economics, students benefit when formal concepts are connected to real policy problems, data, and institutional details.

My approach emphasizes structure, clarity, and practice. I try to make difficult concepts accessible by breaking them into steps, using applied examples, and encouraging students to explain not only what answer they obtain, but why the answer makes economic sense. In empirical and policy-oriented courses, I emphasize interpretation over mechanical calculation and encourage students to understand the limits of causal claims.

I also value inclusive teaching. Students enter economics classrooms with different backgrounds and levels of preparation. A good instructor should set high standards while creating a learning environment where students feel comfortable asking questions, improving through feedback, and building confidence in analytical work.
