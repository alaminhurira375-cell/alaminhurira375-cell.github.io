The Effects of WIC on Household Food Security and the Quality of Food Acquisitions

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 1 / Job Market Paper.

Abstract

We examine the effect of participation in the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) on household food security and the quality of food acquisitions using data from USDA's FoodAPS for 2012–2013. We employ propensity score matching to address biases arising from self-selection into WIC. The Healthy Eating Index is used to measure dietary quality and captures the nutritional adequacy of household food purchases relative to dietary guidance. This study aims to provide new insights into program effectiveness along the lines of “nutrition security,” loosely defined as having both an adequate amount and quality of food. The findings are expected to provide evidence on how WIC affects food insecurity and dietary outcomes among low-income households, carrying important implications for policymakers seeking to improve the design and implementation of nutrition assistance programs.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “The Effects of WIC on Household Food Security and the Quality of Food Acquisitions.” Working paper, University of Georgia.

Dissertation Research

How Would Changes to Community Eligibility Provision Thresholds Affect Child Diet Quality?

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 2 / AAEA 2026 accepted conference paper.

Abstract

The Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) allows eligible schools to provide universal free school meals, increasing access to nutritious food for children in high-poverty communities. In 2023, the U.S. Department of Agriculture lowered the minimum Identified Student Percentage (ISP) required for CEP eligibility from 40% to 25%, expanding the number of schools eligible to participate. This study examines how alternative CEP eligibility thresholds would affect child diet quality. We construct a novel school-level panel of CEP eligibility and participation data from academic years 2014–2015 through 2023–2024 and link these data to school demographic characteristics from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES). To estimate dietary impacts, we combine these data with dietary intake information from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) covering 2013–2023. Following prior research, we estimate individual fixed-effects models that relate Healthy Eating Index scores to the share of calories consumed from school meals and away-from-home food sources, accounting for unobserved child-specific characteristics. We then simulate changes in diet quality under alternative CEP eligibility thresholds by adjusting school meal consumption among students attending schools that would gain or lose CEP eligibility. Preliminary findings suggest that schools near the eligibility threshold account for a modest share of total enrollment but contain student populations particularly vulnerable to reductions in school meal access. Simulations indicate that stricter CEP eligibility requirements would reduce school meal consumption and lower diet quality, with larger effects among disadvantaged students.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “How Would Changes to Community Eligibility Provision Thresholds Affect Child Diet Quality?” Working paper, University of Georgia.

Measuring the Distance to Food Sufficiency: A Continuous Approach to Food Hardship

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. Dissertation Chapter 3 / SEA 2026 accepted conference paper.

Abstract

This study examines food hardship using a continuous measure based on self-reported food expenditure shortfalls from the Current Population Survey Food Security Supplement. Conventional food insecurity indicators rely on discrete classifications that mask variation in the severity of deprivation within categories. Following recent work by Craig Gundersen, we use a resource-gap approach that incorporates the additional dollars households report needing to meet basic food needs. Building on this framework, we construct a normalized needs index by comparing households’ usual food expenditures with their reported shortfalls, allowing for a more interpretable measure of the distance from a food-sufficiency threshold. Using CPS Food Security Supplement data from 2002 to 2023, we examine temporal trends and compare this continuous measure with standard food insecurity indicators. Our analysis highlights differences between binary and continuous measures, particularly during periods of economic change, and reveals substantial heterogeneity in hardship within food-insecure populations. We further explore variation across demographic groups and program participation, including SNAP recipients.

Md Al Amin and Travis Alan Smith. “Measuring the Distance to Food Sufficiency: A Continuous Approach to Food Hardship.” Working paper, University of Georgia.
